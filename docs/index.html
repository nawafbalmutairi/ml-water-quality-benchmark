Comparing Machine Learning Models
for Water Quality Prediction.

A benchmark of Ridge regression, Random Forest, Multi-Layer Perceptron, and XGBoost on 8.3 million UK Environment Agency samples spanning 26 years and 14 regional areas — testing which model best forecasts five core water-quality parameters under a strict chronological train/test protocol.

Water quality monitoring is a slow, costly, and increasingly urgent component of public-health infrastructure. This dissertation benchmarks four machine-learning algorithms — Ridge regression, Random Forest, Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), and XGBoost — on a large UK Environment Agency dataset containing ~8.3 million water-quality samples across five core parameters (Nitrate, BOD, Water Temperature, Dissolved Oxygen, pH), 14 EA regions, and 26 years (2000–2025).

The pipeline survived a mid-project API deprecation in December 2025; ingestion was rebuilt around 364 monthly CSV downloads, batch-renamed by region and year via PowerShell, and verified by a custom script that caught a year-number mismatch in the Thames data before training began.

Under a strict chronological train/test split (train 2000–2017, test 2018–2025), XGBoost emerged as the best overall model, achieving an R² of +0.785 on Water Temperature — the only model × target pair to exceed the +0.7 threshold across all 20 combinations tested. Random Forest and Ridge were competitive on individual parameters but suffered catastrophic failures elsewhere; MLP performed worst overall (avg R² −0.91). The dissertation concludes that tree-based gradient boosting is the most reliable choice for tabular, seasonality-dominated environmental time series, while BOD remains structurally unpredictable from lag features alone — pointing to event-driven biological signals not captured by the feature set.

Introduction.

Clean water is the most basic infrastructure a society has, and yet measuring it remains surprisingly slow. Traditional water-quality monitoring relies on sampling crews, laboratory assays, and statutory reporting cycles that span weeks. By the time the result lands in a regulator's inbox, the conditions that produced it have already changed. This lag is not just operationally inconvenient — it is a public-health problem when contamination events outpace detection.[1]

Machine learning offers a credible second route: predict the quality before the lab result returns. Given enough historical signal — temperature, oxygen, nitrate, biological oxygen demand, pH — a sufficiently powerful model should learn the seasonal, hydrological, and geographical patterns that drive water quality, and forecast the next-step value with useful accuracy. The promise is real; the question is which model.

This dissertation answers that question empirically. It benchmarks four widely-used regression algorithms — Ridge regression (linear baseline), Random Forest (bagged trees), Multi-Layer Perceptron (a small neural network), and XGBoost (gradient-boosted trees) — on a single large dataset published by the UK Environment Agency, and reports per-parameter performance under a fair, chronologically-split evaluation protocol.

Research questions.

  • Which of the four candidate models is most accurate at predicting next-period values for each of five core water-quality parameters?
  • Are some parameters fundamentally easier to predict than others — and if so, why?
  • Can the pipeline be made robust enough to survive upstream data-source changes (e.g. API deprecation) without compromising the integrity of the comparison?

Contributions.

Three contributions are made: (i) a reproducible four-model benchmark on ~8.3M EA samples across 5 parameters and 14 regions; (ii) a hardened ingestion pipeline that survived a real API deprecation event mid-project, with the rebuild documented as a system-design pattern; and (iii) a Power BI dashboard (WQ_ML_Benchmark_Dashboard.pbix) summarising every model × target result for non-technical stakeholders.

Literature Review.

Machine learning for water-quality forecasting is now a crowded subfield with a clear structure: statistical baselines (linear regression, ARIMA), shallow tree ensembles (Random Forest, gradient boosting), and neural approaches (feedforward, LSTM, transformer-based time series). The literature divides cleanly along this spectrum.

Tree-based methods dominate tabular benchmarks.

Multiple studies report XGBoost and Random Forest outperforming linear models on environmental targets. Liu et al. demonstrated XGBoost outperforming SVR and Random Forest for dissolved-oxygen prediction in a Chinese river system.[2] Chen and Guestrin's original XGBoost paper established the algorithm's general efficiency on structured data; subsequent surveys (e.g. Grinsztajn et al., 2022) confirmed that tree ensembles still beat neural networks on most tabular benchmarks.[3][4]

Neural networks are competitive but data-hungry.

MLPs and LSTMs perform well when the training set is large and stationary, but degrade sharply on heterogeneous regional data. Hassan et al. found LSTMs outperformed traditional models on a single-station dataset, but did not extend the test across geographically diverse catchments.[5] This is consistent with the broader finding that neural models trade sample efficiency for representational power — a sometimes-unfair trade when feature engineering has done the work the network would otherwise need to learn.

Linear models remain a floor, not a ceiling.

Ridge regression is rarely the headline winner in modern water-quality papers but appears in almost every benchmark as a baseline. Its purpose here is the same: a transparent, regularised linear floor against which non-linear models must demonstrate justified complexity.[6]

The gap this dissertation addresses.

Despite the volume of work in this area, surprisingly few studies benchmark multiple models on multiple parameters under a single, fair protocol. Most papers optimise one model for one target. This dissertation deliberately holds the data, the preprocessing, and the evaluation protocol constant across four models and five parameters — giving a direct, like-for-like comparison that the existing literature does not provide.

Methodology.

The methodology has four pillars: a chronologically-split evaluation protocol that mirrors real forecasting, identical preprocessing across all four models, per-parameter evaluation using three error metrics (R², RMSE, MAE), and a verification step built into the data pipeline before training begins.

Why chronological, not random?

A random 70/30 split is the default in ML tutorials but is wrong for time-series forecasting. Random splitting allows the model to be trained on future observations and tested on past ones — a form of data leakage that inflates accuracy in a way that does not generalise to real deployment.

Evaluation metrics.

Three metrics are reported for every model × target combination:

  • Coefficient of determination — the fraction of variance the model explains. Ranges from 1.0 (perfect) downward. Negative R² means the model is worse than always predicting the mean.
  • Root mean squared error — penalises large errors quadratically. In the units of the parameter (mg/L, °C, dimensionless for pH).
  • Mean absolute error — penalises errors linearly. Robust to outliers, complementary to RMSE.

Preprocessing held constant.

The same preprocessing pipeline ran for all four models: missing-value handling, type coercion, lag-feature generation, and standardisation. This means architectural performance is the variable; preprocessing is not.

Data & Pipeline.

The data source is the UK Environment Agency's Water Quality Data Archive — a public dataset of statutory water-quality measurements taken across all 14 EA regions from 2000 to the present. The archive contains hundreds of parameters; this study uses five core indicators, all with broad spatial and temporal coverage.

Target parameters.

  • Nitrate (as N) — agricultural runoff indicator, mg/L.
  • BOD: 5 Day ATU — biological oxygen demand, indicator of organic pollution, mg/L.
  • Water Temperature — driver of dissolved oxygen and microbial activity, °C.
  • Dissolved Oxygen — direct indicator of aquatic-life sustainability, mg/L.
  • pH — acid-base balance, affects toxicity of other parameters, dimensionless.

The API outage event.

Mid-project — December 2025 — the EA deprecated the legacy WaterQuality API endpoint. The original Colab ingestion script began returning HTTP 404s overnight. Two choices were available: descope the dissertation, or rebuild ingestion. The rebuild path was taken.

A new beta endpoint was discovered at environment.data.gov.uk/water-quality-beta, with different URL parameters (pointNotation= instead of station=; dateFrom/dateTo instead of date-range bundling) and required an explicit Accept: text/csv header. The pipeline was rebuilt around 364 monthly CSV downloads — 26 years × 14 areas — and standardised via batch PowerShell renaming.

⚠
Verification matters. A custom validation script caught a year-number mismatch in the Thames data — one CSV had been labelled with the wrong year during the rename. Without verification, this would have silently corrupted the chronological split. Treat the break as system design: every component a checkpoint, not a guess.

Discussion.

Why XGBoost wins.

Gradient-boosted trees are well-suited to tabular regression with mixed-distribution targets and rich seasonal structure. XGBoost's sequential residual-correction lets each tree focus on what the previous trees got wrong; combined with its built-in regularisation, this produces strong generalisation on the chronological test set. The result on Water Temperature (R² = +0.785) is a textbook outcome: a strongly seasonal signal, well-captured.

Why MLP fails so badly.

MLP's catastrophic average (−0.91) is driven by two failure modes: Water Temperature at −3.611 and Nitrate at −1.420. Both reflect the same underlying issue — when the input features are already preprocessed into a tabular shape, a feedforward neural network has no advantage to exploit. It pays the cost of being data-hungry without the benefit of representation learning.

The BOD anomaly

BOD is the only target on which every model fails. Ridge "wins" with R² = −0.007 — which is to say, it predicts marginally better than the mean. Random Forest's −3.49 is a sign of overfitting to noisy training signal.

The likely explanation: BOD is driven by acute biological events — sewage overflows, agricultural discharges, storm runoff — that are not encoded in the lag features available to the model. The signal is structurally unpredictable from temporal autocorrelation alone. Improving BOD prediction would require event-driven features (rainfall, treatment-plant capacity, agricultural calendar), not better algorithms.

Random Forest's selective strength.

RF's overall average is dragged into deep negative territory by its catastrophic BOD result, but it actually performs respectably on Water Temperature (+0.729) — second only to XGBoost. This points to a subtle conclusion: RF is a viable choice for a single, well-behaved target, but its variance across parameters makes it a risky default for a generalised pipeline.

Practical implication.

For an environmental monitoring system that must serve multiple parameters from a single pipeline, the right default is XGBoost. Its worst single result (BOD at −0.269) is less catastrophic than the worst case of any other model. The win is not just headline accuracy — it is consistency.

Limitations & Future Work.

Three principal limitations should temper the conclusions of this benchmark.

  • Feature scope. Only autocorrelative lag features are used. External drivers — rainfall, temperature anomalies, upstream point-source discharges, agricultural runoff calendars — are likely to substantially improve BOD prediction and may benefit Nitrate too.
  • Single chronological split. A single train/test cutoff at 2018 was used. A more rigorous evaluation would use rolling-origin cross-validation (e.g. expanding-window with k folds) to estimate stability across multiple historical cutoffs.
  • Hyperparameter tuning. Default hyperparameters were used for all four models. A future iteration with Bayesian optimisation or randomised search per model would establish a tighter ceiling for the neural and tree approaches.

Future directions.

Three avenues for follow-on work emerge naturally from the limitations above. First, integrate external drivers — Met Office rainfall, Defra agricultural records, water-company discharge reports — and re-benchmark BOD. Second, expand to LSTM and Temporal Fusion Transformer architectures to test whether explicit sequence modelling beats the tabular tree approach on multi-step forecasts. Third, deploy the winning XGBoost model as an inference service behind a simple REST API, so the dashboard becomes a live monitoring tool rather than a static report.

Conclusion.

This dissertation answered three research questions empirically. RQ1: XGBoost is the most accurate model overall, winning four of five targets and achieving the only R² above +0.7 in the entire benchmark. RQ2: Water Temperature is the most predictable parameter (strongly seasonal signal); BOD is the least (driven by un-modelled acute events). RQ3: Yes — a robust pipeline survived the December 2025 EA API deprecation and produced a clean, reproducible result; the rebuild process became a system-design pattern rather than a setback.

The headline number — R² = +0.785 for XGBoost on Water Temperature — is the consequence of a methodology that held everything constant except the algorithm. The benchmark is fair because the preprocessing, the split, and the evaluation protocol were identical for all four models. The result has the same shape as the question: which algorithm best learns this data?

For practical deployment in environmental monitoring contexts, XGBoost is the right default: best average performance, lowest worst-case failure, and lowest variance across parameters. Where additional accuracy is needed, the next step is not a different algorithm — it is better features.
